# User Personas & UX Insights: AI-Powered ATS Resume/CV Generator for Indonesian Market

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document defines 6 core user personas for an AI-powered ATS-optimized CV/resume builder targeting the Indonesian market (primary) and global English-speaking market (secondary). Each persona is grounded in observed behavior patterns, cultural contexts, and digital usage realities specific to Southeast Asia. The UX insights section addresses platform-level considerations for building a premium B2C SaaS product targeting 10K+ MAU in Indonesia.

## PERSONA 1: Rina — "The Anxious Graduate"

### Demographics
- **Age:** 22
- **Location:** Yogyakarta / Surabaya (renting a kos-kosan)
- **Education:** S1 (Bachelor's) from a regional university — Universitas Gadjah Mada or Universitas Airlangga — majoring in Management or Communication
- **Monthly Income:** Rp 0–1.5 juta (family-supported, occasional freelance)
- **Tech Literacy:** High on mobile apps and social media, low on productivity/web tools

### Job Search Context
- **Industry:** Targeting entry-level positions in banking, FMCG, or e-commerce (e.g., Bank Mandiri, Unilever, Shopee)
- **Experience:** 0–1 year (internships only, typically MBKM program or campus organizations)
- **Status:** Active searching 3–6 months, has sent 50+ applications, <5 interview calls
- **CV Count:** Has 1 generic CV she uses for everything — and knows it is weak

### Frustrations & Pain Points
- **The "CV Blindness":** She does not know why her applications fail. She gets "no response" but has never had her CV rejected by ATS — she does not even know what ATS is until a friend tells her.
- **Formatting Hell:** Her CV is a Word document she copied from a senior's template. Every time she adjusts margins or spacing, the layout breaks. She has spent 4+ hours on formatting alone.
- **No Mentorship:** Her university's career center only offers generic PDF guides. Her parents cannot help — they worked in different industries. She has no employed senior to review her CV.
- **Imposter Anxiety:** She feels her academic experience (GPA 3.2, no recognizable company names) is "not good enough." She does not know how to phrase organizational experience as professional skills.
- **Language Confusion:** She is unsure whether to write her CV in Bahasa Indonesia or English. Some job posts are in English, some in Bahasa. She switches poorly.

### Goals
- **Primary:** Pass ATS screening and get an interview. Not "a job" — specifically the *first interview* that validates she is employable.
- **Secondary:** Learn *why* her CV fails so she can improve. She wants education, not just a template.
- **Emotional:** Reduce the shame of being jobless 6 months after graduation while her friends are already working.

### Behavior
- **Job Hunting Pattern:** Scattershot — applies to 10–15 jobs per week via LinkedIn, Jobstreet, and Glints, mostly with the same CV.
- **Tools Used:** LinkedIn (finding jobs), Canva (for CV attempts), Google Docs (for draft), WhatsApp (for discussing with friends)
- **Information Sources:** TikTok tips ("CV yang dilirik HRD"), Twitter/Threads threads, YouTube tutorials from Indonesian career influencers
- **Typical Session:** Opens LinkedIn, sees a job, thinks "my CV is not ready," closes app. Avoidance-procrastination cycle.
- **Peer Influence:** Very high. She does what her successful friends did. If a friend used a CV builder and got a job, she will too.

### Device Usage
- **Primary Device:** Smartphone (Xiaomi/Realme/Oppo, mid-range Android, 4–6 GB RAM)
- **Secondary:** Laptop (generic Windows laptop from 2021, slow, used only for "serious" work)
- **Split:** 80% mobile browsing / job discovery, 20% laptop for actual CV editing
- **Internet:** 4G with quota limits (typically 30–50 GB/month, often throttled after quota runs out)
- **Connection Pattern:** Stable at kos-kosan (WiFi), patchy on commute. Often uses "malam-malam" (midnight) quota for heavy tasks.
- **Key Constraint:** Cannot rely on heavy web apps on mobile. Low storage space.

### Willingness to Pay
- **Price Sensitivity:** Extremely high. Rp 0–50k is impulse-buy territory. Rp 50–150k requires thought. Above Rp 150k needs clear ROI proof.
- **Triggers to Pay:** A free version that got her *one* interview. Social proof — "2.000 fresh graduates landed jobs with this." A visible before/after ATS score.
- **Payment Method:** GoPay (most preferred), DANA, ShopeePay, or mobile credit (pulsa). No credit card.
- **Subscription Model:** Prefers one-time or weekly top-up. Monthly subscriptions feel risky — "what if I get a job next week?"
- **What She Will NOT Pay For:** Templates alone (Canva is free). AI cover letter (she thinks it is easy). Premium "tips" (TikTok is free).

### Language Preference
- **Primary:** Bahasa Indonesia, with casual register. She stops reading if the UI is English-only.
- **English Threshold:** Will use English CV builder *only* if the job requires English. Even then, she needs Bahasa Indonesia UI around it.
- **Code-Switching:** Her CV content is mixed — pengalaman organisasi in Bahasa, but "Objective" section she attempts in broken English.

### Feature Priority
| Must-Have | Nice-to-Have | Don't Care |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| ATS score checker | Resume analytics dashboard | Team collaboration |
| AI-guided content suggestions | Multiple templates | API integrations |
| Example phrases for bullet points | PDF export formats | CRM features |
| "Why did my CV fail" explanation | Cover letter generator | Portfolio hosting |
| WhatsApp-sharing ready PDF | LinkedIn profile optimizer | Analytics exports |

## PERSONA 2: Dimas — "The Career Pivot"

- **Age:** 30
- **Location:** Jakarta (Tangerang/Bekasi, commuter)

- **Tech Literacy:** Intermediate — comfortable with SaaS tools, Google Workspace, Notion, but not a power user

### Job Search Context
- **Current Job:** Operations Supervisor at a logistics company (5 years)
- **Target Industry:** Tech / startup — Product Operations, Project Management, or Business Development
- **Experience:** 7 years total, but all in traditional/logistics. Zero "tech" keywords on his CV.
- **Status:** Passive-active — has a job but deeply wants to leave. Applies to 2–3 jobs per month "strategically."
- **CV Count:** Has 1 "master CV" he has been updating for years. It is a dense 3-page wall of text.

### Frustrations & Pain Points
- **Keyword Mismatch:** His current job title is "Supervisor" but he is doing process optimization, vendor management, and data analysis. His CV reads like a logistics person, not a tech PM.
- **The "Translator" Problem:** He knows his skills transfer, but he cannot translate them into tech- industry language. "Mengelola tim 15 orang" should be "Led cross-functional team of 15" — but he does not know that.
- **Age Anxiety:** At 30, he feels "too old" for entry-level tech but "not qualified" for mid-level tech. He worries HR sees his tenure at one logistics company and assumes he is not adaptable.
- **Certification Gap:** His peers in tech have Scrum Master, Product Manager certificates. He has forklift safety training from his logistics days. It looks embarrassing.
- **Ghosted Applications:** He has applied to 40+ tech roles over 2 years. Only 3 interviews. All went nowhere. He suspects his CV is immediately binned by ATS.

### Goals
- **Primary:** Reposition his 7 years of experience as tech-relevant. He needs a CV that tells a *story*, not a chronology.
- **Secondary:** Understand what keywords and formats actual Indonesian tech companies (Gojek, Traveloka, eFishery, etc.) are looking for.
- **Emotional:** Prove to himself (and his skeptical wife) that his career pivot is not a foolish midlife crisis.

### Behavior
- **Job Hunting Pattern:** Strategic, research-heavy. He reads job descriptions thoroughly, customizes his CV slightly (usually fails), then applies via LinkedIn Easy Apply or company career pages.
- **Tools Used:** LinkedIn (primary), Glassdoor (salary research), Glints (for startup roles), Google Sheets (tracking applications), ChatGPT (for rewriting bullet points)
- **Information Sources:** LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, Karir.com articles, Twitter/X tech community
- **CV Editing:** Painfully slow. Each application takes 2–3 hours because he tries to rewrite his experience. He never finishes all the edits.

### Device Usage
- **Primary Device:** Laptop (MacBook Air from 2022, work-provided) — all CV work happens here
- **Secondary:** iPhone 13, mostly for LinkedIn browsing and job alerts
- **Split:** 60% desktop (CV creation, deep work), 40% mobile (browsing, quick applying)
- **Internet:** Stable home WiFi (IndiHome 50 Mbps), stable office WiFi, 4G on commute
- **Key Preference:** Expects desktop-grade UX. Mobile-only is a dealbreaker for serious CV editing.

### Willingness to Pay
- **Price Sensitivity:** Moderate. Rp 100–200k/month is acceptable if the tool demonstrably helps him pivot.
- **Triggers to Pay:** Industry-specific keyword analysis. "Here is what Product Operations roles in Jakarta are looking for — your CV scores 45/100." Resume templates curated for career changers (functional format, not chronological).
- **Payment Method:** GoPay, OVO, Bank Transfer (BCA/Mandiri virtual account). Would also use credit card for convenience.
- **Subscription Model:** Monthly subscription is fine. Willing to pay for 3–6 months (his pivot timeline).
- **What Would Make Him Pay More:** One-on-one AI coaching for career transition. An AI that acts as a career coach, not just a CV formatter.

### Language Preference
- **Primary:** Mixed. He wants Bahasa Indonesia UI but needs English CV output for tech roles. Many Indonesian tech companies use English CVs as standard.
- **Switch Point:** Thinks in Bahasa, outputs in English. The tool needs to accept Bahasa input and generate English CV text naturally.

### Feature Priority

|-----------|-------------|------------|

| Role-specific content suggestions | Cover letter generator | Team features |
| ATS score with keyword gap analysis | LinkedIn profile import | Multi-language CV management |
| Functional/resume format templates | Interview prep tools | Certificate storage |
| "Translate my experience" feature | Salary insights | Video resume |

## UX INSIGHTS FOR INDONESIAN MARKET

### 1. Mobile-First Is Not Optional — It Is The Only Option

Indonesia has 90%+ mobile internet users, but "mobile-first" in the Indonesian context means:

- **Design for mid-range Android devices (4 GB RAM, 720p display).** Not iPhones. Not flagship Androids. The majority of users are on Oppo, Xiaomi, and Samsung A-series priced Rp 2–5 juta.
- **Assume touch-only interaction.** Hover states, right-click menus, and complex drag-and-drop will fail. Every interaction must work with a thumb on a 6-inch screen.
- **Bandwidth-aware design.** The average Indonesian user spends Rp 50–100k/month on data. Heavy assets (images, animations, fonts) increase load time and cost. Critical pages must load under 3 seconds on 4G.
- **Progressive Web App (PWA) is more important than a native app.** Users are storage-conscious. A PWA with offline capability serves the market better than requiring a Play Store download.

**Persona-Specific Tolerance:**
- Fresh Grad (Rina): 5 second max on 4G. Will abandon if it takes longer.
- Corporate (Bu Dewi): 3 second max. Has high-speed internet but low patience for "cheap looking" slow apps.
- Freelancer (Fajar): 7 second max on 4G. Used to slow apps but will churn if it drains his quota.

### 2. The "Gojek Standard" — Indonesian Premium UX Expectations

Indonesian users benchmark your product against the best local apps they use daily: **Gojek, Tokopedia, Shopee, DANA, Traveloka**. These set the bar for:

**Onboarding:**
- Expect a seamless, 3-step-or-less onboarding. No "create account" wall — let them try first (social login via Google or Apple, not email + password).
- OTP verification is standard and expected. But support WhatsApp OTP as well as SMS (SMS delivery in Indonesia can be unreliable).

**Navigation:**
- Bottom tab navigation (like Gojek, Shopee). Not hamburger menus. Not sidebar navigation.
- Precise, action-oriented labels. Indonesian users scan, they do not read. "Buat CV" not "Create New Document."

**Trust Signals:**
- Visible user count ("Sudah digunakan 50.000+ pencari kerja") — this matters enormously.
- Testimonials with real faces. Indonesian users trust other Indonesian users. Western stock photos do not convert.
- Badges or logos of recognizable companies ("Digunakan oleh alumni Gojek, Tokopedia, Traveloka").

**Error Handling:**
- Indonesian users are forgiving of technical issues but unforgiving of rude or unhelpful error messages. "Maaf, ada gangguan. Silakan coba lagi." (Sorry, there is an issue. Please try again.) with a clear retry button.

**Customer Support:**
- WhatsApp-based customer service is expected. Email-only support frustrates users. Chatbots are acceptable if they can escalate to a human.
- Response time expectation: within 2 hours during business hours. Next-day is too slow.

### 3. The Bahasa Indonesia UX Tightrope

**Formal vs. Casual Register:**
- **Do NOT use "kamu" universally.** For fresh grads and freelancers, "kamu" feels friendly. For corporate professionals (Bu Dewi), it feels disrespectful. For tech talent (Adi), it feels unnecessary.
- **Recommended approach:** Default to neutral "Anda" throughout the UI, but allow users to set their preference in profile settings. "Anda" is safe for 95% of users. "Kamu" can be a delightful surprise for younger users if they opt in.
- **Never use "lo/gue"** (Jakarta casual slang) in UI copy. It alienates non-Jakarta users and feels unprofessional even to young users.

**Honorifics:**
- Indonesian users appreciate being addressed as "Bapak" (male) or "Ibu" (female) in confirmation screens, emails, and notifications. This is a subtle but powerful respect signal.
- Example: "Selamat, Bapak! CV Anda sudah siap diunduh." rather than "Selamat! CV kamu sudah siap."

**Tone Calibration by Feature:**
| Feature | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Onboarding welcome | Warm, encouraging | "Yuk, bikin CV yang dilirik HRD!" |
| ATS Score result | Direct but supportive | "Skor ATS Anda 45. Jangan khawatir, ini bisa ditingkatkan." |
| Payment screen | Clear, reassuring | "Pembayaran aman. Data Anda terjaga." |
| Error/empty state | Empathetic, solution-oriented | "Belum ada CV nih. Mulai dari sini, yuk!" |
| Premium upsell | Aspirational, not fear-based | "Buka potensi karier Anda dengan fitur premium." (NOT: "CV Anda jelek, bayar biar bagus.") |

**The "Sopan" Factor:**
- Indonesian professional culture values humility. Overconfident or aggressive copy ("Stand out! Be bold!") feels foreign and off-putting.
- Frame achievements as service and contribution, not self-promotion. "Tunjukkan dampak Anda" (Show your impact) works better than "Show off your achievements."

### 4. Payment Behavior — The Digital Wallet Reality

**Payment Hierarchy in Indonesia (2025):**
1. GoPay (associated with Gojek ecosystem — highest trust)
2. QRIS (universal QR code — accepted everywhere)
3. DANA (strong second)
4. ShopeePay (if user is a Shopee customer)
5. OVO (declining but still relevant)
6. Bank transfer / Virtual account (BCA, Mandiri, BRI)
7. Credit card (<5% of target users have one)

**Pricing Psychology:**
- **Rp 49.000** converts better than Rp 50.000. The "under 50" threshold is powerful.
- **Rp 99.000/month** is the maximum impulse-buy price point for mass market.
- **Annual discounts** are unconvincing in Indonesia due to low credit card penetration and cash flow unpredictability. A "Rp 99.000/month" offer often beats "Rp 599.000/year" even though the latter is cheaper.
- **Weekly or per-use** pricing has no cultural precedent and confuses users. Stick to monthly and one-time.

**Churn Factors:**
- Auto-debit without reminder is a churn accelerator. Send WhatsApp notifications 3 days before renewal.
- "Pause subscription" should be prominent. Indonesian users value control over recurring payments.
- Refund policy must be clear and generous. "Uang kembali dalam 7 hari" (Money back within 7 days) is a strong trust signal.

### 5. Cultural Considerations for AI Conversation

**The AI Assistant Personality:**
- Should feel like a helpful, patient senior (kakak senior or mentor), not a robotic tool and not an overly enthusiastic American-style coach.
- Indonesian users respond well to AI that uses "Kak" (older sibling) as mode of address — it is friendly, respectful, and culturally appropriate for a digital assistant.
- Example opening: "Halo! Aku asisten CV-mu. Aku bakal bantu kamu bikin CV yang dilirik HRD. Mulai dari mana?"

**What to Avoid:**
- Aggressive sales language ("Act now! Limited time!"). This triggers skepticism, not action.
- Overly formal AI that treats the user like a subordinate (common in Indonesian banking apps — users dislike this).
- AI that assumes the user is "desperate" for a job. Frame the interaction as empowerment, not rescue.

**Trust-Building for AI Features:**
- Show the AI's work. Do not just generate content — let the user see bullet points, edit them, and understand why the AI made those suggestions.
- Let users choose the AI's "tone" (Formal / Semi-Formal / Casual). This is a simple toggle that gives users a sense of control over an otherwise intimidating AI interaction.
- Transparency about AI limitations: "AI suggestion — review before using." This manages expectations and builds trust.

### 6. Onboarding, Engagement & Retention Patterns

**Onboarding Completion (The Indonesian Reality):**
- Social login (Google) increases completion rate by 40% vs. email registration.
- A "try before you sign up" flow (upload your CV, get a free ATS score, then register to see results) converts significantly better than a sign-up wall.
- The first "aha moment" must happen within 90 seconds: show the user their ATS score with a clear, actionable insight.

**Feature Discovery:**
- Indonesian users do not explore menus. They use what is in front of them. Key features must be surfaced on the main dashboard, not hidden in settings.
- Tooltips and "coba fitur ini" (try this feature) nudges work well for discovery.
- WhatsApp sharing is the #1 viral channel. A "Bagikan ke teman" button that sends a WhatsApp message with a referral link will outperform all other sharing mechanisms.

**Return Behavior:**
- Users return when there is a clear "next step." If their CV is "done," they will not return until their next job search — which could be 1–3 years.
- To increase return frequency: weekly ATS market insights ("Posisi ini lagi banyak dicari minggu ini"), interview prep tools, salary benchmarking updates.
- The Cover Letter generator is a high-return-frequency feature because users need a new cover letter for each application.

**The "Churn Is Not Failure" Reality:**
- For a CV builder, churn after landing a job is *success*. The product should celebrate this: "Selamat, Anda diterima kerja! Mau pause langganan? Simpan data CV Anda untuk nanti."
- A "save and pause" flow that lets users reactivate their account when they need it next (with all data intact) reduces permanent churn and negative reviews.

### 7. Internet Quality and Performance Constraints

**The Three Speeds of Indonesia:**
| Condition | Latency | Speed | User Proportion |
|-----------|---------|-------|-----------------|
| Urban fiber (Jakarta, Surabaya) | <20ms | 50+ Mbps | ~20% |
| Urban 4G (most cities) | 30-80ms | 5-20 Mbps | ~60% |
| Peri-urban / Rural 4G | 80-200ms | 1-5 Mbps | ~20% |

**Design Implications:**
- Lazy load everything. A fresh grad on a quota-capped connection will abandon if the page weighs >2 MB.
- Images should be WebP format, aggressively compressed.
- Fonts: use system fonts (Inter, Noto Sans) rather than custom fonts where possible. Each custom font weight = additional load.
- Offline capability for draft editing (PWA service worker) is a competitive advantage, especially for commuters who open the app on KRL Commuter Line or TransJakarta where signal is patchy.

### 8. Competitive Landscape Signals

Indonesian users have been burned by:
- **Free "CV builder" sites** that watermark the output or hide the download behind a paywall after hours of work.
- **Scam recruitment platforms** that collect CV data for spam. Data privacy concerns are real and justified.
- **Cookie-cutter templates** that produce CVs looking identical to thousands of other applicants.

**What Builds Trust:**
- Transparent pricing (no hidden fees)
- Free ATS scan with no account required (viral sharing opportunity)
- Data deletion policy that is easy to find and execute
- Partnerships with known Indonesian job platforms (Karir.com, Glints, Jobstreet) or universities
